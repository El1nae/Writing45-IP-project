How did Bacchanalia revive in 2126?

And how do we recover our sincerity as it used to be?

Publish Time: 2127-05-16 | Category: News | Views: 766227

One year ago, on June 9th, millions of people flooded into streets, bars, and public squares to celebrate Bacchanalia for the very first time in modern history. And honestly? Nobody saw it coming — that this ancient festival, pieced together from scattered literary references and dusty historical archives, would take over the world this fast.

Now, with the second Bacchanalia of 2127 quietly creeping up on us, cities across North America, Europe, and Asia are already getting loud about it. If you haven't started preparing yet, don't panic — you still have time.

Restaurants are draping grapevines across their doorways and setting out silver goblets. Students are writing holiday cards to their closest friends in class. And on social media, Bacchanalia content is absolutely everywhere. If you don't want to miss the wave, make sure you're using the tags:

#June9   #Bacchanalia   #EverydayHolidaY

For a lot of people, the holiday already feels so familiar — as though it had never been forgotten.

Here's a little story I feel like sharing.

Around this time last year, I went to invite a friend of mine to celebrate Bacchanalia with me. She's one of the rare few I've met who actually pushed back on the whole thing. She looked at me and said:

"Why are you so sure today is Bacchanalia? Why should we even celebrate it? It sounds like a purely Greek holiday!"

I laughed and gave her the most irresponsible, most honest answer I had: because June is so boring.

Seriously — what does June have going for it? In winter, you can ride Christmas, Halloween, and New Year's back to back without coming up for air. But June? There's barely a reason to gather, to spend, to feel like anything special is happening. Malls are empty of holiday promotions. No streamers, no decorations, no gift exchanges. Just a long, flat stretch of days that I'm pretty sure every single person on Earth has felt at some point.

She wasn't convinced, though.

She still wanted to push back against this trend — this thing we had finally reclaimed in an era of spiritual emptiness.

And I think, perhaps, that's exactly why Bacchanalia was revived in 2126 in the first place. It was a gift people reclaimed for themselves. If we stop valuing sincerity and a little human warmth, our future is going to get very cold and very rational very fast.

Her doubts are just one small episode, of course. The origins and history of Bacchanalia have been verified across multiple sources.

✦   ✦   ✦

According to last year's historical research, Bacchanalia was formally established as an annual celebration on June 9th — a date that closely lines up with the period when grapevines enter their flowering and pollination stage. In traditional viticulture, early June has long been considered a critical window that determines whether fruit can successfully form, and it's carried symbolic weight around vitality and emotional release for centuries.

June 9th also falls near the summer solstice. The days are at their longest. The nights are no longer cold. In that kind of gentle darkness, it's not hard to imagine people in earlier times staying up to celebrate until dawn — which is why many scholars believe this is a key reason why ancient Bacchanalia became so stubbornly tied to nighttime festivities, music, drinking, and collective revelry.

Over the past year, the idea of "briefly escaping the grind" has spread rapidly among younger generations — especially against a backdrop where brutal work culture and prolonged emotional suppression have gradually become the norm. More and more people have started treating Bacchanalia as the one moment that "actually makes them feel alive."

That energy has pushed young people to express themselves more openly, and to show up more honestly in front of each other.

And somewhere along the way, people started to realize: Bacchanalia had become more than a holiday. It had become a new social language.

✦   ✦   ✦

But there's a current worth paying attention to beneath all of this.

Across enormous amounts of social media content, "whether or not you participate in Bacchanalia" has quietly become a way of measuring whether someone is genuine, open, and willing to be real with their friends. This collective mindset has been nudging young people toward an unspoken conclusion: anyone who won't let loose, even briefly, must not be sincere enough.

Sentiments like this showed up in festival-related videos and party invitations last June. Some people started assuming, almost as a default, that truly close friends should drink together, stay up all night, talk until sunrise, and do things they'd never ordinarily do — all on Bacchanalia. And that's what none of us would like to see.

Those who chose not to join were often called "killjoys" or "unnatural."

In post-festival interviews conducted last year, many young people admitted they hadn't actually wanted to go — but went anyway because they "didn't want to ruin the mood for everyone." One student who attended last year's Bacchanalia night in Los Angeles put it plainly:

"Everyone was so excited for that day. If you were the only one to say no, it felt like you were single-handedly destroying the whole thing."

This kind of pressure has gradually been reframed as a form of "freedom."

We should always remember: Bacchanalia isn't about indulgence — it's about briefly escaping the state of being controlled by your own life.

Within that framing, choosing not to join the revelry can just as well be a personal choice. Nobody should feel bound or pressured into anything on this day — and that, perhaps, is what Bacchanalia was always truly about.

✦   ✦   ✦

New waves of consumption have risen around this sense of ritual. Limited-edition Bacchanalia badges, grapevine brooches, and custom silver cups all sold out almost entirely around last year's festival.

Over the past year, a persistent question has followed the holiday: how could a festival only recently rediscovered exert such swift and sweeping influence over an entire society?

For the many people already counting down to the second Bacchanalia, though, that question seems to matter less and less.

This week, New York, Tokyo, Paris, and Seoul have all announced their all-night programs for June 9th. Some cities are planning to open "Eve of the Solstice districts" for the first time, allowing businesses to stay open until sunrise.

On social platforms, the most widely shared phrase heading into this year's Bacchanalia is:

"If you're going to fall every once in a while —
why not on Bacchanalia?"
